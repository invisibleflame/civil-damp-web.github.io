CE102-Engineering Mechanics


It is the first course of civil core taught to students in the first year

1

2014-2015

Review 1 (By Rishi Vanukuru)

Prof. Yogesh M. Desai
Well to begin with the course is largely an extension from the basics of mechanics that all of us have had to learn over 11th and 12th. In fact in the first class, Yogesh sir listed down the basic formulae on the board and stated ‘You now know everything you need to know about this course!’. So the reason why we do have the course is not so much content building as it is learning how to apply it in more real-life situations. It is very numerical/practice oriented, with minimal theory as such.
It has largely been constant across years, repeating what was said in an earlier review here for convenience : Planar and parallel forces, Equivalent Force couple systems, Wrench, Distributed forces, Equation Of Statics, Friction, Trusses and cables, Virtual Work Method , Potential Energy Method, Vibrations-equation of motion, degrees of freedom, Damping, Harmonic Oscillators.
None, it is the DIC after all.
Well personally I found it to be a very enjoyable course, largely because the content was something we all had studied before, and because the Professor was really enthusiastic about the course and understood the class well. While he is very strict about certain issues (like carrying the course booklet and calculator to every class), it never is a burden, and over time everyone understands why it’s important. The classes had solving sessions incorporated, and tutorials at times for added practice. The TAs were really helpful too, and that made things a lot easier. Attendance rules do apply (>80% I guess), though I believe this is one course where attending every class/tutorial session would go a long way to understanding the problem solving techniques required to solve the problems in the quizzes and exams. It also is the first course offering some sort of insight into what the department has to offer, which is why among the courses in the semester this is the one with most long term significance and hence do give it the required amount of attention and effort.
In our year it was a 10-30-10-50 split across Quiz 1, the Midsem, Quiz 2 and the Endsemester examinations. The tests were all closed book, no additional notes allowed. They were largely dependent on applications in the real world, and not just brute force solving.
I guess it’s as much as you make it to be. If you are in touch with JEE level mechanics and do practice regularly, the course will be really easy and fulfilling. If not, well a lot of hard work would be required, as this is one of the more competitive courses.
I had referred Vector Mechanics by Beer and Johnston, and the textbook on engineering mechanics by I.H.Shames. That coupled with the course booklet (it has a large range of problems as well) and past year papers are more than sufficient material to get you through the course well.
Reiterating, be regular if you can and make sure to carry the course booklet and a calculator (He really does send people out without exception). Being the first class conducted in the department gives a nice feel to it, with the entire section sitting together and all. On the whole it was one of the more fun courses of that semester, so as long as you don’t take it too lightly you should be fine.
